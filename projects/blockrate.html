BlockRate Retired

A group-buying marketplace for recurring home services in Austin. Neighbors pooled for volume pricing; providers got route density. It worked, technically. I killed it anyway.

The Model

Lawn care costs $120 a visit when you buy it alone. BlockRate let neighbors pool: at five-plus households on one block, the price fell to $66 a visit, providers got dense routes instead of scattered one-offs, and the platform took 20%. Group-buying economics applied to the most recurring purchase a homeowner makes.

What I Built

This was the most technically serious product in the portfolio — a real two-sided marketplace, launched across four Austin regions and forty-seven neighborhoods, with at least one paying customer:

  • Money that moves correctly Stripe Connect payouts direct to providers, Stripe Checkout and Billing Portal for customers — with dynamic tiered repricing that recalculated every member's rate on every join or cancellation.
  • Rate protection Thirty-day price locks so a neighbor's cancellation didn't jolt your bill mid-month — the trust mechanic that makes group pricing tolerable.
  • Operational plumbing most MVPs skip Scheduled visit generation with epoch-aligned frequencies, a PGMQ email queue with dead-letter handling via Resend, a Block Champion referral program, a Contentful blog, and admin impersonation for support. Thirteen edge functions, fifteen-plus tables, row-level security throughout.
Why I Killed It

Three reasons, none of them technical.

Wrong customer. Cost-conscious consumers hunting discounts are high-churn and price-defecting — every retention lever fights their core motivation for being there. No sector edge. I don't know the home-services industry from the inside, so every provider-side judgment was secondhand. The go-between trap. A two-sided marketplace makes you responsible for both sides' failures: provider no-shows became my refunds; customer churn became everyone else's price jumps.

Sophistication of build ≠ viability of business. Building was never the bottleneck — choosing the right problem and customer is. The lesson that reshaped this portfolio

Lessons

One paying customer doesn't validate a business if the category of customer is wrong. BlockRate is why everything after it targets builders and operators paying for leverage — and why this portfolio treats a well-reasoned shutdown as a shipped outcome. Killing your most sophisticated work because the business underneath it is wrong: that's the PM judgment I'd hire for.
