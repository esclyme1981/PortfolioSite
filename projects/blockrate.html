BlockRate

KILLEDREVENUE

Group-buying for recurring home services: neighbors pooled for volume pricing, providers got route density, the platform took 20%. It worked – technically. This page is about why I shut it down anyway.

the model

Lawn care runs $120 a visit solo. Pool five-plus households on one block and BlockRate priced it at $66 – providers got dense routes instead of scattered one-offs, and the platform took 20%. Group-buying economics on the most recurring purchase a homeowner makes. On paper, elegant. On paper.

why it died

  • It turned me into a customer-service rep. Every provider no-show, reschedule, and refund landed in my inbox. The job stopped being "build the marketplace" and became "apologize between two parties, daily."
  • The supply side wasn't tech-savvy. Providers ran their businesses on phone calls and paper. Every feature I shipped needed a phone call to explain – you can't automate a side that doesn't want software.
  • Wrong customer. Cost-conscious consumers hunting discounts are high-churn by definition – every retention lever fights their reason for being there.
A working product with revenue can still be the wrong business. Reading that signal in months – not years – is what experience looks like. the one-line lesson that reshaped everything after it

lessons

Three paying customers don't validate a business if the category of customer is wrong. BlockRate is why everything after it targets builders and operators paying for leverage – and why a fast, well-reasoned shutdown counts as a shipped outcome in this directory. The tech wasn't the point and never is: the point is how quickly you recognize "not right" and act on it. That speed is the judgment I'd hire a PM for.
